Week 9: Final Project Update 2

Introducing Lenses

My final project topic has changed.

For the past 4 months, I have been participating in the development of an NYC Media Lab project called Lenses. Lenses is intended to be web tool with which citizens and journalists create easily build embeddable interactive graphics using uploaded datasets and/or data from open API sources. Currently, we are focusing development on open government and civic-based APIs.

Purpose and notable features of Lenses

  • Lenses is a free, open-source, publicly available web-based tool that will allow for the WYSIWYG creation of data visualizations and interactive graphics by citizens, journalists, and bloggers.
  • Lenses is a tool for both data exploration and presentation. The MakeLenses interface is divided into three steps: 1.) Data acquisition, 2.) Data Exploration, 3.) Data Visualization. We are developing functionality for each step in the process. (Our UI hasn’t been developed yet, but the basics can be found here: Linear Version, I/O version, collaborative repository.
  • Lenses workspaces encourage collaboration and fact-checking by preserving the steps taken to create each visualization. Specifically, the Lenses workspace records information on provenance (copied form html table, API call, file upload, etc), it keeps a visual record of data transformations (ex. group, filter, sort, split, etc), and it allows for forking, sharing, and multi-user editing.
  • Lenses is extensible and developers will be able to add new components written HTML, JS, and CSS.

This tool was conceive in the spirit of encouraging engaged citizenship, fostering data literacy, and promoting civic and government transparency.

My Role

To date, my role has not involved much creative coding. I have agreed to assist in developing lenses for labor- and health-related data, and have been investigating various data sources. I have also met with students from Columbia’s School of Journalism to better understand their data visualization needs, such as:

  • where do they look for data sources,
  • how do they obtain data,
  • what difficulties to they encounter and which steps of the process would benefit most from automation,
  • and how open datasets have affected their practice.

We are developing each Lens a Web Component, using Goggle’s Polymer framework. The framework has taken a little while to get my head around, but I think the immense utility of web components makes the undertaking worthwhile.

Very soon, I plan to mock-up several visualizations in Processing and then translate them to P5.js. The development of visualizations will be my creative contribution to this project and will draw directly from skills learned in this class. After I have working web-based visualizations, I will integrate my code into the Lenses framework to test for usability.

I will post sketches very soon… so stay tuned.

P.S. - I will be presenting the projects with the rest of the Lenses team at the Poly Research Expo on April 24.

Week 9: Final Project Update 1

In preparation for the final project that I proposed on March 3rd, I’ve done a significant amount of reading on vocal acoustics and how they are measured. While the topic of my final project has changed (see Week 9: Final Project Update 2), I wanted to note the most helpful resources I found.
I hope to continue this project at a later date.

SW Phonetics

http://swphonetics.com/

This is a blog written by Sidney Wood, a professor emeritus in phonetics at the University of Lund and University of Umea, Sweden. Wood does an excellent job of explaining the fundamentals of speech waveforms and how they are measured; formants, anatomy, and vocal resonance, and the basics of spectroscopy, FFT, and LPC parameters for speech.

BTW, human speech waves are incredibly complex and computational phonetics could get pretty freaking cool.

Praat


Praat Software
Code and Tutorials on Computational Phonetics: http://www.fon.hum.uva.nl/praat/

This is free, open-source software developed by Paul Boersma and David Weenink, professors of phonetic sciences at the University of Amsterdam, The Netherlands. Their website assumes users know the basics of speech analysis (which I do not); however it is useful in providing an in-depth reference on the Praat software and speech measurements.

The Scientist and Engineer's Guide to Digital Signal Processing

by Steven W. Smith, Ph.D. http://www.dspguide.com/pdfbook.htm

This online book seems like an excellent reference on DSP tech and all the algorithms, jargon, and special techniques of its sub-disciplines (sound, imaging, radar & sonar, etc.) You can download individual chapters as .pdfs. I downloaded Chapter 22 on audio signal processing, and found the Speech Synthesis and Recognition section helpful.

The Stanford Center for Computer Research in Music and Acoustics (CCRMA)

https://ccrma.stanford.edu/~jos/sasp/

This site is intended for musicians, however it provides excellent explanations of the math behind things like the Hamming windows; cepstral windowing and linear prediction; FFT, IFFT, STFT and peak detection, and Levinson–Durbin recursion. I do not anticipate that an understanding of math behind these functions should be necessary to complete the proposed project, but it is good to know there is a reference for the calculations called in AudioManager.cpp of our current iOS app build.

This site also introduced me to Gnu’s Octave (it's like open-source Matlab), which may come in handy in the future.

Week 2: Sol LeWitt Wall Drawing #305

The Eighth point is located halfway between the two points where an arc with its center at the first point and with a radius equal to the distance between the first and the seventh points would cross a line from the upper right corner to a point halfway between the midpoint of the bottom side and the lower right corner.

Explanation from MoCA:

Wall Drawing 305 is composed of one hundred random specific points that are determined by the draftsman. The points are random in that they may be placed anywhere on the wall. The draftsman uses Sol LeWitt’s vocabulary and geometric lexicon to guide the mapping of the points. This lexicon includes the corners, midpoints and center of each wall, which serve as reference points that are connected and traversed by lines and arcs. The one hundred points are specific in that they are created at the meeting of the junctures of these formal elements. As the draftsman maps out each generated point, he or she writes a description of how he or she arrived at that point next to it. This allows the viewers to trace the process of the placement of the points.

Wall Drawing 305 is one of a series of drawings in which LeWitt experimented with textual instructions that direct the draftsman to construct shapes on the wall. Called ‘location’ drawings, these works are done in black pencil with geometric figures emphasized in crayon, foregrounding the process of drawing as a problem-solving mechanism.

Source: http://www.massmoca.org/lewitt/walldrawing.php?id=305
